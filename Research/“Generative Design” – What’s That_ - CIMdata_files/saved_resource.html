            Cheryl Peck Mod Sachin Galgalikar • 3 years ago

            Sachin,

            Generative design (GD) has been used in aerospace applications for over two decades now. In the early days the tools were shape and topometry optimization, topology optimization came a little later.

            This paper from Airbus is nine years old, but it certainly shows that major companies have embedded this technology into the way they design their products.

            https://www.researchgate.ne...

            Indeed, at some companies the application name has become a verb: "Did you Optistruct that part?"

            The next time you are on a newish commercial airplane, take a few minutes to look at the design. The frame of your seat, the cover, latch, and hinges on the overhead luggage compartment, and the bulkheads that separate you from first class. It is likely that GD has been used to design all of them.

            The archetypal aerospace GD part is a wing rib:

            View Hide

            The top and bottom of the image support the airfoil shape of the wing.The large holes are for spars that support the length of the wing, and the smaller holes are for electrical, hydraulic, and fuel networking. Note the stiffening ribs. The structure needs to be strong and stiff enough, but also of minimum weight. I suspect a GD solution for this part would be dramatically different than the design in the illustration.

            The exciting thing now is that with new manufacturing methods, parts can be much more 3D in their geometry - a wing rib is basically a 2D structure.

            You can find much more on the web. Search on a company name (Boeing, Airbus, GE Aircraft, ...) and terms like optimization, topology, light weight.

            Best,

            Keith

                  Cheryl Peck Mod DavidChadwick • 3 years ago

                  David,

                  Yes, the geometry output from current Topology Optimizers is usually some kind of finite-element mesh or tesselated surface. At best it is a body-fitted STL triangulated surface. At worst, it is voxels, uniform cubes.

                  I believe that TopOpt should, at some level, use the same mathematics for geometry as the CAD system: NURBS. The difficulty of taking TopOpt results into usable CAD is perhaps the biggest obstacle in the Generative Design (GD) process. The availability of "Convergent Modeling" in Parasolids, which makes faceted surfaces a CAD b-rep data type, is an improvement, but not a solution.

                  B-rep (boundary representation) is the definition of CAD solid objects by their boundary surfaces. In the Generative Design world that some of us envision, this is insufficient. GD will specify the interior structure of the part, be it varying materials and properties, or an internal lattice that has engineered properties. So far as I know, no CAD system has a way to represent properties or microstructure (varying in space and direction) in the interior of a solid.

                  Keith
